## Part B – Social Media Do's and Don'ts

The full checklist is saved as `social-media-checklist.md` in this folder.

### Quick Reference

| ✅ Do's | ❌ Don'ts |
|---|---|
| Keep your LinkedIn profile updated and professional | Don't post anything you wouldn't want a future employer to see |
| Think before you post — ask: "Would I be comfortable if my professor saw this?" | Don't share personal information like your address, phone number, or daily schedule publicly |
| Use privacy settings to control who sees your content | Don't engage with or spread unverified news and rumours |
| Credit others when sharing their work or ideas | Don't use social media to bully, mock, or publicly shame someone |
| Report harmful or suspicious content using platform tools | Don't accept connection/friend requests from people you don't know or verify |
| Keep your college email separate from personal social accounts | Don't post during exams, internships, or professional events without thinking |
| Log out of accounts on shared/public devices | Don't share OTPs, passwords, or banking details with anyone online |

## 📝 Reflection

The most eye-opening part of this task was writing the internship email. I realised how easy it is to accidentally sound either too casual ("Hey, I wanted to ask about an internship...") or strangely robotic and over-formal. Getting the tone right — professional but genuine — took more thought than I expected.

One situation that stood out to me while researching poor digital communication: in 2021, a student at a university in Pune shared an angry post about a professor on a public group chat. The message was meant for a small group of friends but was screenshotted and forwarded widely. It resulted in a formal disciplinary hearing and damaged both the student's and the professor's reputation.

What could have been done differently? The student should have either addressed the issue privately with the professor, spoken to a counsellor, or if venting was necessary, done it in a private one-on-one message rather than a group chat. This incident reinforced for me that once something is digital, it is permanent and shareable — and no platform is truly "private."
