## Quick Reference

| &nbsp;&nbsp;✅&nbsp; **Do This** | &nbsp;&nbsp;❌&nbsp; **Never Do This** |
|:---|:---|
| ✔️ Keep your **LinkedIn** and **GitHub** profiles updated and professional | 🚫 Post anything you wouldn't want a **future employer** to see |
| ✔️ Ask yourself: *"Would my professor, parent, or employer be okay seeing this?"* | 🚫 Share your **address, phone number, or daily schedule** publicly |
| ✔️ Use **privacy settings** to control who sees your content | 🚫 Engage with or spread **unverified news and rumours** |
| ✔️ **Credit others** when sharing their work, memes, or ideas | 🚫 Use social media to **bully, mock, or publicly shame** anyone |
| ✔️ **Report** harmful or suspicious content using platform tools | 🚫 Accept **friend or connection requests** from people you can't verify |
| ✔️ Keep your **college email** separate from personal social accounts | 🚫 Post casually during **exams, internships, or professional events** |
| ✔️ **Log out** of all accounts on shared or public devices | 🚫 Share **OTPs, passwords, or banking details** with anyone online |

## 📋 Full Checklist

*Use this before posting, connecting, or sharing anything online*

</div>

---

### 🟢 SECTION 1 — Building a Positive Digital Presence

```
□  Keep LinkedIn and GitHub profiles updated, complete, and professional at all times
□  Use a consistent, real name across professional platforms (GitHub, LinkedIn, Kaggle)
□  Review your own profile from a stranger's perspective every 3 months
□  Add achievements, projects, and certifications as soon as they happen
□  Keep a professional profile photo on LinkedIn — no cropped group photos
```

---

### 🔵 SECTION 2 — Posting and Sharing Responsibly

```
□  Before every post, ask: "Would I be comfortable if my professor saw this?"
□  Check the source of any news or claim before sharing — one share can spread misinformation
□  Always credit original creators when sharing their work, art, or ideas
□  Avoid vague "callout" posts — if you have an issue, address it directly and privately
□  Never post in anger — wait 24 hours before publishing anything emotionally charged
□  Avoid posting your real-time location — share photos after you have left
```

---

### 🟣 SECTION 3 — Privacy and Account Security

```
□  Set Instagram, Facebook, and X to private if used for personal content
□  Review app permissions — social media apps should not access your microphone at all times
□  Use a strong, unique password for each social platform — never reuse passwords
□  Enable two-factor authentication (2FA) on every account
□  Log out of all accounts on shared computers, phones, or public devices
□  Keep your college email exclusively for academic platforms — never mix with personal
□  Regularly audit which third-party apps have access to your social accounts
```

---

### 🔴 SECTION 4 — Protecting Yourself from Online Threats

```
□  Never accept friend or connection requests from accounts you cannot verify
□  Never share OTPs, PINs, or passwords — even with people claiming to be "support"
□  Do not click links sent via DMs from unknown accounts, even if they look real
□  Never agree to "verify" your account through a link sent in a private message
□  Block and report accounts that send suspicious messages or make you uncomfortable
□  Do not share screenshots of private conversations in public groups or stories
```

---

### 🟠 SECTION 5 — Professional and Academic Conduct

```
□  Do not post negative comments about professors, classmates, or your institution publicly
□  Avoid controversial posts on platforms linked to your real name
□  Do not post during exams, internships, or formal events — it signals disrespect
□  Be careful with humour — what feels like a joke to you may hurt others
□  Never impersonate anyone online, even as a "prank"
□  Do not use your college affiliation to make claims that aren't officially sanctioned
```

## 🚨 Real Scenario — A Cautionary Tale

</div>

> **What Happened:**
> In 2021, a student at a university in Pune vented about a professor in what they assumed was a private WhatsApp group of 40 classmates. One member screenshotted the message and forwarded it. Within hours it had reached the department head. The student faced a formal disciplinary hearing, and the professor's reputation was also impacted — despite having never seen the original message themselves.

> **What Should Have Been Done Differently:**
> The student should have either spoken directly with the professor, raised the concern through the institution's grievance process, or vented privately in a one-on-one conversation with a trusted friend — not a group chat. No group is truly private in the digital world.

> **The lesson:** Every message you send can be forwarded. Every post can be screenshotted. Act accordingly.

</div>

```
🔑  THINK  →  Would I be okay if this was public?
```
